In the Sri Lankan context, there is an urgent need to address this complexity, as it contributes directly to both a high snakebite burden and the unnecessary loss of biodiversity through the killing of snakes.1.1 Problem to be addressedSri Lanka is lack of an effective, real time and accessible method for snake identification. The comparative analysis of existing research reveals several critical gaps in snake species identification systems. Most studies suffer from limited dataset diversity, often focusing on a narrow range of regional species, which restricts model generalizability. The data quality is frequently compromised due to reliance on online images or controlled environments, lacking real-world context. So, a method that can be used to identify the snakes correctly in an event of a snake encounter will be a valuable support for the people, as well as for the snake enthusiast that is interested in the area of herpetology.1.2 Objectives of the projectIn our study, we propose a real time identification method of snakes in an event of an encounter, to address the problem above. The objectives of the proposed study are,* To prepare a dataset containing images of different snakes.* To implement and optimize a preprocessing algorithm that considers camera distortions and light conditions of input image.* To integrate and adapt a segmentation method to extract the snake and the background separately.* To develop the deep learning model for identification of snakes.* To develop a tool to visualize the result processed by the model to the end user.* Creating a database to keep track of snake images and information.1.3 Project DeliverablesProject deliverables mentioned below are the outcomes that are expected to achieve as the results of this study.1. The snake segmentation method.2. The deep learning model for identification.3. The Mobile application for real time identification of snakes.4. The technical reports.1.4 System design approachAgile methodology prioritizes iterative development and collaborative problem-solving to deliver functional increments. And, the processes which we are using in the system. There are several algorithms and processes related to our system. In this section we describe the main processes and the main algorithms that are important to our system.2.4.1 Forward Propagation AlgorithmForward propagation is the process where input data travels through the neural network's architecture, moving sequentially from the input layer toward the output layer. During this phase, neurons in every layer compute weighted sum of their inputs, execute specific activation functions, and transmit the resulting values to the subsequent layer. This continuous flow culminates in the production of a final output, which serves as the network's prediction for the provided data.2.4.2 Error Calculation AlgorithmThis stage involves using a mathematical loss function to determine the numerical difference between the networkŐs predicted result and the actual target value, known as the ground truth. Common metrics used for this measurement include Cross-Entropy Loss or Mean Squared Error ($MSE$). The resulting error value acts as a performance indicator, providing the necessary data to inform and drive the modelŐs optimization process.2.4.3 Backward Propagation AlgorithmBackpropagation identifies how much each weight and bias contributes to the total error by calculating gradients in reverse, starting from the output and moving toward the input layer. By applying the mathematical chain rule, the system determines the specific impact of every parameter on the overall discrepancy. These calculated gradients are then utilized by optimization techniques, such as gradient descent, to refine the weights and biases. The primary objective of this iterative adjustment is to minimize the loss and enhance the model's predictive precision.2.4.4 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)CNNs are specialized architectures consisting of several functional layers designed for feature extraction and classification:* Convolutional Layer: Utilizes a series of learnable filters to detect and extract significant patterns or high-level features from the input.* Activation Layer: Implements non-linear functions, such as Rectified Linear Unit ($ReLU$), to allow the model to learn complex patterns.* Pooling Layer: Downsamples the data to decrease spatial dimensions, which lowers computational requirements while preserving essential information.* Fully Connected Layer: Acts as the final stage of the network to produce the ultimate regression or classification results.2.4.5 Geospatial Contextual FilteringTo improve accuracy, the system employs a contextual filtering algorithm using GPS data.1. Location Capture: The app retrieves the user's current latitude and longitude.2. Contextual Matching: The predicted species is cross-referenced with the known geographical distribution of that snake in Sri Lanka.If a predicted species is not native to the user's current location, the system may flag the result for lower confidence or suggest alternative species common to that region.2.5 State machinesState machines are behavioral diagrams used to model the dynamic lifecycle of a single object or a system. While a flowchart describes a sequence of activities, a state diagram focuses on the states  an object occupies and how it moves between them in response to specific events.2.5.1 Account RegistrationAfter the user submits the registration form, the account status is initialized as 0, indicating a pending or inactive state. Many snakebite incidents occur due to fear, panic, and the inability of people to correctly identify snake species. Misidentification often leads to unnecessary killing of harmless snakes or delays in seeking proper medical treatment. Therefore, there is a strong need for a technological solution that can assist people in identifying snakes quickly and accurately.With the widespread use of smartphones and recent advancements in artificial intelligence and machine learning, mobile applications have become powerful tools for solving real-world problems. Image recognition technology, particularly deep learning, has shown high accuracy in object classification tasks. This research proposes the design and development of a mobile application for real-time snake identification using machine learning techniques.This Research Design document explains the overall plan, structure, methods, tools, and procedures used to carry out the research. It provides a clear roadmap of how the research objectives are achieved, from data collection to system evaluation.5.1 Study objectives-based literature review The literature review for this research is conducted by aligning existing studies with the core objectives of developing a mobile application for real-time snake identification using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Previous research in the domain of computer vision highlights CNNs as one of the most effective deep learning techniques for image-based classification tasks. CNNs have been successfully applied in areas such as wildlife monitoring, medical imaging, and object recognition due to their ability to automatically extract hierarchical features from raw image data.Several studies demonstrate the effectiveness of CNN architectures such as VGGNet, ResNet, and MobileNet in classifying biological species. Among these, MobileNet has gained significant attention for mobile and embedded applications because of its lightweight architecture and reduced computational complexity. Research also emphasizes the importance of preprocessing techniques, such as normalization and image augmentation, to enhance model accuracy under varying lighting and environmental conditions. However, despite these advancements, limited research focuses specifically on real-time snake identification in mobile environments, particularly in regions like Sri Lanka where snake-human encounters are frequent. This study builds upon existing literature by proposing a real-time, mobile-friendly CNN-based solution aimed at improving public safety and awareness.5.2 Formalizing high-level implementation components The proposed system is designed using a high-level modular architecture to ensure efficiency, scalability, and maintainability. The system consists of four primary components: the mobile application interface, image preprocessing module, CNN-based snake identification model, and the result visualization and information module. Each component plays a distinct role while maintaining seamless communication with other modules.The mobile application functions as the primary interaction point for users, allowing them to capture or upload images during a snake encounter. Once an image is provided, it is forwarded to the preprocessing module, which prepares the image by resizing, normalizing, and correcting distortions caused by camera quality or lighting conditions. The processed image is then passed to the CNN model, which performs feature extraction and classification. The final output, including snake species details, venom status, and confidence level, is presented to the user through a clear and intuitive interface. This component-based design ensures real-time responsiveness and future extensibility of the system.5.3 Data extractions, sample design, test data sets, training data sets The dataset used in this research consists of labeled images of snake species collected from publicly available datasets, research repositories, and verified online sources. Data extraction involves selecting images that accurately represent snake species under different environmental conditions. Images with poor quality, incorrect labels, or excessive noise are excluded to ensure dataset reliability.The selected dataset is divided into training, validation, and testing data sets. The training dataset is used to train the CNN model and contains the majority of the images. The validation dataset is used during the training phase to monitor model performance and adjust hyperparameters, helping to prevent overfitting. The test dataset is kept separate and used only for final performance evaluation. To enhance model robustness and generalization, data augmentation techniques such as rotation, flipping, zooming, and brightness adjustment are applied. These techniques help the model adapt to real-world variations encountered during mobile usage.5.4 Non-functional aspects The non-functional aspects of the proposed system describe the quality attributes that influence the overall performance, reliability, and efficiency of the mobile application for real-time snake identification. These aspects are critical to ensuring that the system operates effectively in real-world conditions while maintaining accuracy and responsiveness.CNN Architecture Performance and ReliabilityThe design of the Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture focuses on achieving high classification accuracy and reliability. System performance is evaluated by comparing predicted snake species with verified ground truth data. Standard evaluation metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score are used to measure the effectiveness of the model. Particular emphasis is placed on the reliable distinction between venomous and non-venomous snake species, as incorrect classification could lead to serious safety risks. The architecture is designed to minimize misclassification while maintaining stable and consistent prediction performance.Dataset Diversity and Model RobustnessThe system design incorporates a diverse and representative dataset to ensure robustness and generalization. The training dataset includes images of Sri Lankan snake species captured under varying environmental conditions, such as different lighting levels, backgrounds, angles, and snake postures. This diversity allows the CNN model to learn discriminative features that remain effective across real-world scenarios. Data augmentation techniques are integrated into the design to reduce overfitting and enhance the modelŐs ability to perform reliably in unseen conditions. Model Efficiency and Optimization for Mobile DeploymentEfficiency and optimization are essential non-functional considerations due to the mobile nature of the application. The CNN model is designed to be computationally lightweight to ensure fast inference and low latency on mobile devices. Transfer learning techniques using optimized architectures such as MobileNet are adopted to balance accuracy and processing speed. The design minimizes computational overhead to reduce battery consumption and avoid excessive processing delays, ensuring smooth real-time performance without compromising user experience.5.5 Proposed validation methods and measurementsThe proposed system is validated using both quantitative and qualitative evaluation methods. Quantitative validation includes standard classification performance metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. These metrics provide a comprehensive assessment of the CNN modelŐs effectiveness in identifying snake species. A confusion matrix is used to analyze correct and incorrect predictions and to identify potential misclassification trends.In addition to classification accuracy, system response time is measured to evaluate real-time performance on mobile devices. The model is tested using unseen images from the test dataset to simulate real-world conditions. Qualitative validation involves user-based testing to assess ease of use, clarity of results, and overall system satisfaction.
